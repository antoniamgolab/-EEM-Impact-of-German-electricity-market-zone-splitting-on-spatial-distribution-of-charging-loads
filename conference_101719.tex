\begin{abstract}
The objective of this paper is to quantify the impact of a potential German electricity bidding zone split on the spatial distribution of charging infrastructure required for long-haul battery-electric trucks. Splitting Germany's unified zone into a wind-rich North and a demand-heavy South would create persistent regional price differentials, altering where along a route it is cheapest to charge. We assess this effect with a cost-optimization model for fleet turnover and charging allocation along the Scandinavian--Mediterranean Trans-European Transport Network (TEN-T) freight corridor. Results show that while aggregate battery-electric truck (BET) adoption is insensitive to the split, spatially varying adoption concentrates more BET activity in the North, and the spatial allocation of charging demand shifts substantially northward. This redistribution propagates beyond German borders, affecting required charging capacity in neighboring countries. Total corridor-wide capacity remains constant across all scenarios, confirming that the zone split redistributes---but does not reduce---aggregate demand. These findings highlight that electricity market zone design creates infrastructure planning externalities for the transport sector that extend across national borders.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Motivation}

Across Europe, there is an ongoing discussion on whether large, uniform electricity bidding zones should be reconfigured toward smaller zones that better reflect local pricing signals \cite{b_moretto2026, b_knorr2025}. This debate is driven by persistent grid congestion that arises when uniform wholesale prices fail to reflect the spatial mismatch between generation and demand. As renewable generation---particularly wind power---concentrates in specific regions, the mismatch between generation and demand creates systematic congestion patterns that uniform pricing cannot resolve, leading to costly redispatch interventions and inefficient investment signals.

One of the most prominent instances of this debate is the potential splitting of the German electricity market zone~\cite{b_trepper2015, b_fraunholz2021, b_knorr2025, b_moretto2026}. Germany currently operates as a single bidding zone despite significant structural congestion along its North--South axis: wind-rich northern regions generate surplus electricity that must be transmitted to demand-heavy southern industrial centers~\cite{b_trepper2015, b_fraunholz2021, b_knorr2025, b_hurta2022, b_ostergaard2025, b_emelianova2025}.

Simulation studies show that a German North--South split reduces redispatch but creates significant distributional effects on electricity prices, generation investment, and welfare~\cite{b_trepper2015, b_fraunholz2021, b_knorr2025, b_moretto2026}. Beyond the electricity sector, zone reconfigurations cascade into end-use sectors: they alter the feasibility of electrification in heating, transport, and industry~\cite{b_ostergaard2025, b_emelianova2025}, change investment viability of storage infrastructure~\cite{b_hurta2022}, and affect demand differently across economic sectors~\cite{b_cho2015}.
A consumer sector rarely discussed in this context is the transport sector. Charging costs---largely determined by local electricity prices---significantly affect the cost competitiveness of battery-electric trucks~\cite{b_noll2022, b_lanz2022}, yet the effect of price differences across electricity market zones on the spatial allocation of charging demand has barely been studied, with the exception of Pagnier et al.~\cite{b_pagnier2026} who find a relevant link between zonal electricity prices and optimal truck electrification in Norway. Unlike their single-country analysis, the present study examines cross-border spillover effects along an international freight corridor.
Moreover, existing charging infrastructure planning tools for long-haul truck freight do not account for spatially varying electricity prices along the route, implicitly assuming uniform charging costs across regions and countries~\cite{b_shoman2023}.

An added dimension in the transport sector, compared with stationary consumers such as industry or heating, is that transport consumers are mobile: long-haul trucks travel across multiple regions and, for international freight, across national borders and electricity market zones. This mobility creates an opportunity---when charging is optimized over the route---to exploit spatial price differentials by shifting charging demand toward regions with lower electricity costs, thereby improving cost competitiveness. It also means that a zone split in one country (here, Germany) can redistribute charging demand not only within that country but along the entire transit corridor.


\subsection{Research Objective}
This leads to the following research questions: \textbf{(1)~How does a splitting of the German electricity market zone affect the spatial distribution of truck charging demand between Northern and Southern Germany? (2)~Does this redistribution propagate beyond German borders, affecting required charging infrastructure capacities in neighboring countries along the corridor?}

We address this by simulating a market split via expected electricity price levels under different conditions of the split (spread magnitude and timing), deriving location-specific charging costs, and modeling the long-term demand response with a linear program for cost-optimal multi-year fleet turnover and charging demand allocation along origin-destination truck flows on the Scandinavian--Mediterranean TEN-T corridor. This corridor is selected because it traverses Germany along its full North--South axis, crossing both proposed electricity market zones, and extends into neighboring countries on both sides, enabling the analysis of cross-border spillover effects.

\section{Methodology and Data}

\subsection{German market zone splitting}

We simulate the impact of a German electricity market zone split by constructing a scenario matrix that varies two dimensions: (i)~the \emph{magnitude} of the North--South electricity price spread, and (ii)~the \emph{timing} of when the zone split takes effect. The scenario matrix is summarized in Table~\ref{tab:scenarios}.

\subsubsection{Scenario design}
A single \emph{baseline} scenario assumes a unified German bidding zone throughout the modeling horizon (2020--2050), corresponding to the current market design. Six zone-split scenarios are constructed by combining three spread levels with two implementation timings (from~2032 and from~2040).

\textbf{Price spread levels.}
The price differential between the North and South German zones is applied as a symmetric, relative adjustment to the baseline wholesale electricity price at each node:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Low (5\%):} North $-2.5\%$, South $+2.5\%$ --- conservative, based on early estimates by Trepper et al.~\cite{b_trepper2015}
    \item \textbf{Mid (10\%):} North $-5\%$, South $+5\%$ --- central estimate, consistent with Kn\"orr et al.~\cite{b_knorr2025} and Fraunholz et al.~\cite{b_fraunholz2021}
    \item \textbf{High (20\%):} North $-10\%$, South $+10\%$ --- upper bound, reflecting early-period spreads in Fraunholz et al.~\cite{b_fraunholz2021}
\end{itemize}
Using a relative (percentage-based) differential ensures that the absolute price spread scales with the price level: larger in early years when wholesale prices are higher, and smaller as prices decline with renewable energy expansion.\footnote{For the High (20\%) scenario, the resulting fast-charging price spread (including CPO markup) between North and South Germany is approximately 5.2~ct/kWh in 2030 and 2.9~ct/kWh in 2040; for Mid (10\%) it is 2.6 and 1.5~ct/kWh; for Low (5\%) it is 1.3 and 0.7~ct/kWh, respectively. For a BEV truck consuming $\sim$1.2~kWh/km over a typical $\sim$600~km German transit segment, the High scenario translates to a cost difference of roughly EUR~37 per trip in 2030 and EUR~21 in 2040 between charging entirely in the North versus entirely in the South.}

\textbf{Implementation timing.}
The zone split takes effect in one of two years:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{From 2032:} prices remain unified until 2031; split from 2032 onward
    \item \textbf{From 2040:} prices remain unified until 2039; split from 2040 onward
\end{itemize}
Before the split year, all German nodes retain the baseline (unified) electricity price. This dimension captures the policy uncertainty around \emph{when} such a reform might be implemented, and whether early versus late splitting has different effects on the already-established truck fleet and charging infrastructure.

\textbf{Zone definition.}
Germany is divided into two zones following the transmission system operator (TSO) boundary convention used in Trepper et al.~\cite{b_trepper2015} and Fraunholz et al.~\cite{b_fraunholz2021}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{North} (50Hertz + TenneT-North): wind-rich regions with lower marginal generation costs (Lower Saxony, Schleswig-Holstein, Mecklenburg-Vorpommern, Brandenburg, Berlin, Saxony, Saxony-Anhalt, Thuringia, Bremen, Hamburg)
    \item \textbf{South} (Amprion + TenneT-South + TransnetBW): demand-heavy regions with higher marginal costs (NRW, Hesse, Rhineland-Palatinate, Saarland, Baden-W\"urttemberg, Bavaria)
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Electricity prices and charging costs}
Baseline wholesale electricity prices are taken from the \textbf{Go~RES} scenario of GENeSYS-MOD~\cite{b_genesys}, which assumes ambitious renewable energy expansion in line with the EU's Green Deal targets. This scenario is selected because it represents a central pathway with high renewable penetration---precisely the conditions under which North--South price differentials in Germany are expected to be most pronounced due to the geographic concentration of wind power in the North. Go~RES provides annual wholesale electricity prices by country for 2020--2050 across all corridor countries (NO, FI, SE, DK, DE, AT, IT). For zone-split scenarios, the German prices are adjusted as described above: for each German node, the wholesale price is multiplied by $(1 - \delta/2)$ in the North zone or $(1 + \delta/2)$ in the South zone, where $\delta$ is the relative spread (5\%, 10\%, or 20\%). This adjustment is only applied from the split year onward; earlier years retain the unified price. A charge point operator (CPO) markup of 30\% is added to wholesale prices to account for grid fees, operating costs, and profit margins, yielding the final fast-charging electricity price at each location.

Fig.~\ref{fig:elec_prices} shows the resulting electricity charging prices (including CPO markup) for scenario H-2032. The North--South differential is clearly visible: DE-North prices fall below the corridor-country range while DE-South prices rise above it. Non-German countries remain unaffected by the split.

\subsection{Cost-optimization of fleet turnover and charging demand allocation}

The spatial allocation of charging demand and fleet composition is determined by a linear program that minimizes total discounted system cost over the horizon 2020--2050 with a 4-year time step. The model, TransComp~\cite{b_transcomp}, is formulated as a network design problem and determines, for each origin-destination (OD) pair and period, the cost-optimal mix of internal combustion engine vehicle (ICEV) and battery-electric vehicle (BEV) trucks, the spatial allocation of charging energy along routes, and the required charging infrastructure investments. Table~\ref{tab:nomenclature} summarizes the key notation.

\subsubsection{Objective function}
The objective minimizes the net present value of all costs:
\begin{equation} \label{equ:obj}
    \min_{\mathbf{x}}\; z = \sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} d_y \,\Delta \Big( C^{\mathrm{veh}}_y + C^{\mathrm{fuel}}_y + C^{\mathrm{toll}}_y + C^{\mathrm{travel}}_y + C^{\mathrm{charg}}_y \Big)
\end{equation}
where $d_y = (1+\rho)^{-(y-y_0)}$ is the discount factor and $\Delta$ is the period length (4~years). Vehicle costs $C^{\mathrm{veh}}$ include CAPEX of new purchases and age-dependent maintenance. Fuel costs $C^{\mathrm{fuel}}$ include location-specific energy prices and a carbon surcharge for fossil fuels: $\sum_{r,t,g,e} s_{yrtge} \cdot ( p^{\mathrm{fuel}}_{te}(y) + \varepsilon_t \cdot \pi^{\mathrm{CO_2}}_e(y))$. Toll costs $C^{\mathrm{toll}}$ are technology-differentiated distance-based road charges. Travel time costs $C^{\mathrm{travel}}$ monetize only the \emph{deviation} from baseline travel time (driving + mandatory breaks) using a value of time, so that charging during mandatory driver rest incurs no additional cost. Charging infrastructure costs $C^{\mathrm{charg}}$ cover CAPEX, O\&M, and grid connection charges for deployed capacity.

\subsubsection{Key constraints}

\textbf{Demand coverage.} All freight demand must be served:
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{t,g} f_{yrtg} = F_{yr} \quad \forall\, y, r
\end{equation}

\textbf{Fleet turnover.} The vehicle stock is tracked by generation through a stock-flow balance of existing vehicles, retirements (after fixed lifetime $\Lambda_{tg}$), and new purchases, capturing the fleet inertia that constrains the pace of technology transition.

\textbf{Technology substitution rate.} The rate at which the fleet composition can change between consecutive periods is bounded:
\begin{equation}
    \left| H^{t}_{y} - H^{t}_{y-\Delta} \right| \leq \alpha_h \cdot H^{\mathrm{total}}_{y} + \beta_h \cdot H^{t}_{y-\Delta} \quad \forall\, y > y_0,\, t
\end{equation}
where $H^{t}_{y} = \sum_{r,g} h_{yrtg}$ is the corridor-wide fleet of technology $t$ at year $y$, $\alpha_h$ is the maximum share of the total fleet that any technology can gain per period, and $\beta_h$ bounds growth relative to the technology's own previous stock. Both parameters are set to 0.1. Additionally, BEV purchases are blocked before~2028 to reflect realistic market introduction timelines.

\textbf{State of charge.} The battery state of charge is tracked spatially along each route:
\begin{equation}
    soc_{yrtge_{i}} = soc_{yrtge_{i-1}} + s_{yrtge_{i}} - D^{\mathrm{spec}}_{tg} \cdot L_{e_{i-1,i}} \cdot \frac{f_{yrtg}}{W_{tg}}
\end{equation}
with initial SOC set to $SOC^{init} \cdot Q^{\mathrm{batt}}_{tg}$ and a minimum final SOC constraint. The SOC must remain within $(0, Q^{\mathrm{batt}}_{tg})$ at all intermediate nodes, preventing stranding and ensuring feasibility of BEV operation. This constraint is the key mechanism through which electricity prices affect spatial charging allocation: the optimizer distributes charged energy $s$ along the route to minimize fuel cost subject to the battery capacity envelope.

\textbf{Charging infrastructure.} Installed capacity (initial + cumulative expansion) must meet peak demand at each location:
\begin{equation}
    Q^{\mathrm{charg}}_{e} + \sum_{y' \leq y} q^+_{y'e} \;\geq\; \gamma \sum_{r \in \mathcal{R}_e} \sum_{t,g} s_{yrtge} \quad \forall\, y, e
\end{equation}
where $\gamma$ converts annual energy to peak power demand.

\subsection{Traffic flow data}

The model operates on OD freight flows at the Nomenclature of Territorial Units for Statistics (NUTS) level~2 along the Scandinavian--Mediterranean TEN-T corridor, covering Norway, Finland, Sweden, Denmark, Germany, Austria, and Italy. Freight volumes are derived from the ETISplus European freight transport database~\cite{b_speth2021}, which provides bilateral road freight tonnages between NUTS-2 regions. Routes are computed as shortest-path sequences of NUTS-2 regions over the highway network, and mandatory driver rest stops are allocated along each route following EU~Regulation~561/2006 (4.5~h maximum continuous driving time)~\cite{b_transcomp}.

Two route categories are included:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{International routes passing through Germany:} cross-border OD pairs whose path transits through at least one German NUTS-2 region (e.g., IT$\leftrightarrow$DE, SE$\leftrightarrow$DE, IT$\leftrightarrow$SE via DE). International routes that bypass Germany entirely (e.g., AT$\leftrightarrow$IT, DK$\leftrightarrow$NO via SE) are excluded since they are unaffected by the German zone split and would produce identical results across all scenarios.
    \item \textbf{German domestic N--S routes:} OD pairs where both origin and destination are in Germany but in \emph{different} electricity market zones (North$\leftrightarrow$South). These routes are essential for the zone-splitting analysis because trucks traveling within Germany across the zone boundary can reallocate charging between cheaper (North) and more expensive (South) regions.
\end{enumerate}

Routes are filtered to include only those with a minimum path length of 360~km (corresponding to $\sim$4.5~hours of driving at 80~km/h, i.e., one mandatory driver rest interval), a minimum freight volume of 500~tonnes/year in the base year, and transit through at least one German region.
After filtering, the dataset contains approximately 1,787 international OD pairs transiting through Germany and 83 German cross-zone domestic OD pairs, for a total of $\sim$1,870 routes. The 249 international routes bypassing Germany (e.g., AT$\leftrightarrow$IT, Scandinavian intra-Nordic) are excluded to reduce computational cost without affecting results.

\section{Results}

We present results for all seven scenarios, focusing on route-level electrification shifts, spatial redistribution of charging demand, and cross-border infrastructure spillovers.

\subsection{Route-level electrification shifts}

While the aggregate BEV fleet share remains nearly identical across all scenarios ($\sim$43.7\% at 2040), the zone split fundamentally reshapes \emph{which} routes electrify. Fig.~\ref{fig:violin} shows the distribution of route-level BEV share changes between scenario H-2032 and the baseline at~2040, disaggregated by corridor type. Routes are classified as \emph{North only} if their path passes exclusively through DE-North NUTS-2 regions, \emph{South only} if exclusively through DE-South, and \emph{cross-zonal} if through both.

North-only routes gain on average $+18$~percentage points (pp) in BEV share, with many routes shifting from $\sim$15\% to 100\% BEV---corresponding to approximately 1,160 additional tonnes transported by BEV trucks on these corridors. Conversely, South-only routes lose on average $-10$~pp, with $\sim$2,820~tonnes remaining on ICEV instead of switching to BEV. Cross-zonal routes show a more balanced but mixed effect (mean $+2$~pp), with 212~routes gaining and 95~losing more than 5~pp BEV share, as these routes traverse both cheaper and more expensive zones.

\subsection{Spatial redistribution of charging demand}

Fig.~\ref{fig:charging_delta} shows the change in annual charging demand relative to the baseline for each zone-split scenario. The redistribution is approximately symmetric: charging demand gained by DE-North mirrors the loss in DE-South. The effect scales with the price spread---scenario H-2032 shifts nearly 500~GWh/a northward by~2048, while even scenario L-2032 produces a shift of $\sim$270~GWh/a. Later implementation (from~2040 vs.\ from~2032) reduces the shift but does not eliminate it, as the cumulative fleet and infrastructure decisions taken before the split constrain the system's response.

\subsection{Infrastructure utilization and cross-border spillovers}

The zone split creates a mismatch between where capacity is built under baseline assumptions and where it is actually needed. Fig.~\ref{fig:used_unused} compares installed capacity utilization for the baseline and scenario H-2032. In DE-South, the split causes significant overcapacity (hatched bars) during the 2028 and 2036 investment periods as demand migrates northward, though this overcapacity diminishes over time as the system adjusts. In DE-North, the opposite occurs: required capacity exceeds the baseline build-out, creating a deficit that must be met by accelerated infrastructure investment.

\section{Discussion and Conclusion}

Regarding the first research question, results show that the zone split does not alter aggregate BEV adoption but fundamentally reshapes \emph{where} charging demand arises. Lower electricity prices in the North zone lead the cost-optimization to allocate more BET activity to the North and pull charging demand northward, while DE-South experiences a corresponding decline. The effect scales with the price spread magnitude and is stronger when the split is introduced earlier, as more fleet turnover decisions occur under differentiated prices.

Regarding the second research question, the redistribution of charging demand does not stop at German borders. In the most pronounced scenario (H-2032), Austria loses up to 31~MW of required charging capacity relative to the baseline, as the large North--South price differential pulls demand back into cheaper DE-North. Italy, at the southern end of the corridor, sees a moderate increase. These cross-border spillovers show that a unilateral zone-split decision in one country creates infrastructure planning externalities for transit countries along the same freight corridor.

These results point to two broader conclusions. First, they indicate the need for cooperative planning between electricity market designers, transport policy makers, and infrastructure planners to effectively avoid significant misallocation of charging infrastructure. Without coordination, infrastructure investments risk being stranded in regions where demand declines after a zone split, while underserving regions where demand grows. Second, the strong sensitivity of charging demand allocation to local electricity prices emphasizes that spatially varying charging costs need to be considered endogenously in infrastructure planning models, rather than treated as a uniform input.

The results also indicate temporary overcapacities in DE-South when infrastructure built under unified-zone assumptions faces reduced demand after the split. A rolling-horizon formulation, where infrastructure investment decisions are made with limited foresight, would better capture these overcapacity risks and provide more realistic estimates of stranded assets when the market design changes unexpectedly. Extending the analysis to additional corridors and zone configurations beyond the two-zone North--South split would further generalize the findings.
